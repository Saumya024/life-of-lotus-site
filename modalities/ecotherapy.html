Ecotherapy

Lifestyle, Preventive & Nature-Based Practices

How It Works

  • Therapeutic activities are conducted in natural settings or incorporate nature
  • Approaches range from structured programs to simply spending time outdoors mindfully
  • A therapist may guide the work or nature itself may be the primary healer
  • Activities include forest bathing, gardening, animal-assisted therapy, and wilderness programs

What People Typically Experience

  • Calming effects of natural environments
  • Reduced anxiety and depression symptoms
  • Improved concentration and creativity
  • Sense of perspective and connection to something larger
  • Physical benefits from outdoor activity

Who This Is For

  • People who feel better in nature
  • Those with nature-deficit in their daily lives
  • Anyone seeking alternatives to office-based therapy
  • People drawn to outdoor and environmental healing

Frequently Asked Questions

Is ecotherapy just being outside?
It's more intentional than casual outdoor time. Structure, guidance, or therapeutic framing distinguishes ecotherapy from general nature exposure.
What is forest bathing?
A Japanese practice (shinrin-yoku) of immersing in forest atmosphere through slow walking and sensory engagement. Research supports its health benefits.
Can ecotherapy help with depression?
Research supports nature exposure for mental health. For clinical depression, it's often most effective as part of broader treatment.
Do I need to be outdoorsy?
No. Ecotherapy adapts to your comfort level. Even urban nature—parks, gardens—offers benefits.
What about people who can't access nature?
Bringing nature indoors (plants, sounds, images), virtual nature experiences, and urban green spaces can provide some benefits.
Is this evidence-based?
Growing research supports ecotherapy. Japanese shinrin-yoku research is particularly substantial.
