How It Works

  • You formulate a question and cast coins or yarrow stalks to generate a hexagram
  • The hexagram (six-line figure) is looked up in the I Ching text for its meaning
  • Changing lines may indicate a second hexagram showing how the situation evolves
  • The answer is contemplated and applied to your specific question

What People Typically Experience

  • Surprisingly relevant responses to their questions
  • A different perspective on situations they were too close to see clearly
  • Guidance that becomes more meaningful over time
  • A sense of dialogue with something wiser than their conscious mind
  • Clarity emerging gradually through contemplation

Who This Is For

  • Those interested in Chinese philosophy and practical wisdom
  • Anyone seeking a contemplative practice for gaining perspective

Frequently Asked Questions

How can throwing coins give meaningful answers?
The I Ching philosophy suggests that random processes can reflect underlying patterns. Whether you see it as synchronicity, unconscious guidance, or simply a contemplative tool, many find the responses remarkably apt.
Do I need to learn Chinese or study for years?
No. Many excellent translations exist with clear explanations. You can begin consulting the I Ching immediately and deepen understanding over time.
What is the difference between coins and yarrow stalks?
Both generate hexagrams, but yarrow stalks give slightly different probability distributions for changing lines. Coins are simpler; yarrow is more traditional. Results from both are valuable.
Can I ask any question?
Questions work best when they're sincere inquiries rather than tests. Ask about things you genuinely need guidance on, and phrase questions to invite wisdom rather than demand predictions.
How often should I consult the I Ching?
Traditionally, asking the same question repeatedly is discouraged. Ask once, contemplate the answer, and return only when circumstances have genuinely changed.
Is the I Ching connected to any religion?
It emerged from Chinese philosophical tradition. It's not specifically religious and is used by people of all backgrounds.
