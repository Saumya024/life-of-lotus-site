Music Therapy

Expressive & Creative Therapies

How It Works

  • A trained music therapist uses music interventions to address therapeutic goals
  • You may listen to music, create it, move to it, or discuss responses
  • No musical skill is required—the therapy adapts to your abilities
  • Specific techniques target physical, emotional, cognitive, or social needs

What People Typically Experience

  • Emotional access and release through musical engagement
  • Improved mood and reduced anxiety
  • Memory access for those with cognitive impairment
  • Social connection through group music-making
  • Physical benefits like improved movement in rehabilitation

Who This Is For

  • People who respond strongly to music
  • Those with dementia or cognitive impairment
  • People in rehabilitation from injury or stroke
  • Anyone seeking non-verbal therapeutic approaches

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need musical ability?
No. Music therapy works with any level of musical experience. The therapy adapts to you.
What happens in a session?
Depends on your goals. Might include listening, singing, playing instruments, songwriting, or moving to music.
Is this the same as listening to relaxing music?
Music therapy is structured by a trained therapist toward specific goals. It's more focused than general music listening.
Can music therapy help with depression?
Research supports its effectiveness for depression and other mental health conditions.
Is music therapy evidence-based?
Yes, significant research supports it for various conditions including dementia, stroke recovery, mental health, and pain.
How do I find a qualified music therapist?
Look for board-certified music therapists (MT-BC in the US). Professional organizations can help locate practitioners.
