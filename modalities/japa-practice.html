Japa Practice

Sound, Mantra & Vibrational Practices

How It Works

  • A mantra is repeated using counting beads (a mala with 108 beads typically)
  • The practice is done for a specific number of rounds or time period
  • Focus is on the sound, its meaning, or the sensation of repetition
  • Practice may be whispered, spoken aloud, or purely mental

What People Typically Experience

  • Deep meditative states through rhythmic repetition
  • Reduced anxiety and mental quieting
  • Sense of devotion or connection to the sacred
  • Building of spiritual energy over time
  • Greater focus and clarity extending to daily life

Who This Is For

  • People who like structured, countable practice
  • Those drawn to devotional approaches
  • Anyone seeking a portable meditation method
  • People who find open meditation too unstructured

Frequently Asked Questions

Why 108 beads?
108 is considered sacred in many traditions. The specific number isn't essential—the consistency of practice matters most.
How is japa different from mantra practice?
Japa is a specific way of doing mantra practice—with counting beads and often specific numbers of repetitions.
How long does a round take?
Depends on the mantra. A round of 108 typically takes 5-15 minutes. Many people do multiple rounds daily.
Can I do japa anywhere?
Yes, it's highly portable. Some people practice during commutes or waiting times. Dedicated quiet time is ideal but not required.
What if I lose count?
Most teachers say to simply continue. The counting supports focus but isn't the goal. Don't stress about exact numbers.
Do I need special beads?
Any mala works. Traditional materials (rudraksha, tulsi) are valued, but what matters most is consistent practice.
