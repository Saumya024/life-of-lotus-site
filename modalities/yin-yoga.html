Yin Yoga

Yoga, Breath & Embodied Disciplines

How It Works

  • Poses are held for 3-5 minutes (or longer), typically on the floor
  • Muscles are relaxed, allowing stress to transfer to deeper connective tissues
  • The practice targets fascia, ligaments, and joints rather than muscles
  • Stillness and discomfort are part of the practice—you learn to be with what is

What People Typically Experience

  • Deep release in hips, lower back, and spine
  • Improved joint mobility over time
  • A meditative quality that calms the nervous system
  • Access to emotional layers stored in the body
  • Better flexibility in connective tissue, not just muscles

Who This Is For

  • People who feel tight despite stretching muscles
  • Athletes wanting to complement intense training
  • Those seeking meditative practice through the body
  • Anyone dealing with stress held in hips or lower back

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Yin Yoga relaxing?
It's not about relaxation—it's about being present with sensation, which can be intense. The nervous system calms, but the practice requires attention.
Can Yin Yoga hurt me?
When practiced correctly, no. The key is finding appropriate sensation—not too intense, not too mild. Never go to pain.
Why hold poses so long?
Connective tissue requires sustained, gentle stress to change. Short holds don't give fascia time to release.
Can I do Yin every day?
Most teachers recommend 2-4 times weekly. Too much can overstress tissues. Balance with more active practices.
Is this good for hypermobile people?
Yes, but with modifications. The focus shifts from going deeper to building stability within the range you have.
How is this different from Restorative Yoga?
Restorative is about complete relaxation with props supporting you. Yin involves active engagement with moderate intensity.
