Nada Yoga

Sound, Mantra & Vibrational Practices

How It Works

  • Practice involves listening for subtle internal sounds
  • External sounds may be used as portals to inner listening
  • Techniques progress from outer hearing to increasingly subtle inner sounds
  • The ultimate practice is resting in the source of all sound—silence

What People Typically Experience

  • Heightened sensitivity to sound
  • Deep states of meditation through listening
  • Experience of inner sounds—ringing, humming, music
  • Profound stillness as listening deepens
  • Unity of outer and inner through sound

Who This Is For

  • People with natural sensitivity to sound
  • Those seeking meditation through listening rather than focusing
  • Musicians and sound artists interested in inner dimensions
  • Anyone curious about consciousness through auditory awareness

Frequently Asked Questions

What are inner sounds?
Subtle sounds that emerge in stillness—often described as bells, flute, thunder, or buzzing. They're not imaginative but directly perceived.
Is this related to tinnitus?
Some inner sounds differ from tinnitus, which is typically constant. Nada yoga works with sounds that emerge in deep listening.
Do I need musical training?
No musical background is needed. It's about deep listening, not musicianship.
How do I find a teacher?
Nada yoga is less common than other practices. Look for teachers in traditional yoga or tantra lineages, or start with recorded guidance.
Can this help with noise sensitivity?
Paradoxically, working with sound consciously often reduces reactivity to noise. The relationship with sound changes.
How long until I hear inner sounds?
Some people hear them quickly; others take time. Regular practice in quiet environments helps.
