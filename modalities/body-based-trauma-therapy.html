Body-Based Trauma Therapy

Somatic & Nervous-System-Based Practices

How It Works

  • The therapist works with physical sensations and body patterns, not just talking about experiences
  • Trauma stored in the body is accessed through awareness of tension, posture, and physical responses
  • Various techniques may be used—breathwork, movement, touch, or simply tracking sensations
  • The body's wisdom guides the pace and direction of healing

What People Typically Experience

  • Release of physical tension connected to traumatic memories
  • Completion of defensive responses that were interrupted
  • Greater sense of safety in their own body
  • Reduction in trauma symptoms like hypervigilance or numbness
  • Integration that feels more complete than talk therapy alone

Who This Is For

  • People whose trauma shows up physically—chronic pain, tension, or symptoms
  • Those who've done talk therapy but still feel something's stuck in their body
  • Anyone whose body 'knows' things their mind hasn't processed
  • People seeking thorough, embodied healing from trauma

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this a specific technique?
It's an umbrella term for approaches that work with the body in trauma treatment—including Somatic Experiencing, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, and others.
Do I have to be touched?
Not necessarily. Many approaches work with awareness and movement without physical touch. If touch is used, it's with clear consent and boundaries.
Can this retraumatize me?
Skilled practitioners know how to work slowly and safely. The risk is lower than approaches that push for catharsis or detailed narrative.
How is trauma stored in the body?
Trauma can create patterns of tension, restricted breathing, altered posture, and nervous system dysregulation that persist long after the event.
Will I have to relive the trauma?
No. Body-based approaches typically don't require detailed recall. They work with present-moment body experience, which is often less overwhelming.
How long does this type of therapy take?
It varies widely based on trauma history and individual factors. Many people notice shifts within months; complex trauma may require longer.
